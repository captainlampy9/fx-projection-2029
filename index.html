Looking at this series, two points stand out. First, VND depreciating against USD is a long-term trend, but it is a controlled depreciation — not a freefall. Second, the pace of depreciation is not linear: some years it rises fairly quickly, some years it moves sideways, and in 2021 the annual average actually declined slightly. From 21,698 in 2015 to 24,165 in 2024, cumulative increase is approximately 11.4%, equivalent to roughly 1.2% per year annualized. This is the most important historical 'drift rate' for anchoring expectations.

Looking at end-of-period rates instead of annual averages: ADB shows USD/VND rising from 21,890 at end of 2015 to 24,335 at end of 2024 — also approximately 1.18% per year compound growth. In simple terms: over the past 10 years, USD has gained against VND in trend terms, but gained gradually.

Current Market Level & Recent Macroeconomic Data

As of early April 2026, NCB quotes the interbank average rate at 25,106 VND/USD and the USD selling price at 26,361; Vietcombank also displays a USD selling price of 26,361 updated on April 6–7, 2026. This means the actual transaction market is already in the 26,300 range for bank selling rates — significantly higher than the 2024 statistical average.

At the same time, the latest NSO data shows Q1/2026 GDP grew 7.83% year-on-year, while Q1/2026 average CPI rose 3.51%, and March 2026 CPI alone rose 4.65% year-on-year. This is very important: it proves Vietnam has not fallen into a state of macroeconomic weakness, but price pressures are still edging up and the exchange rate is no longer as cheap as in 2023–2024. In other words, the current state is: growth remains strong but VND is under mild depreciation pressure — not yet a currency crisis.

Scenario Analysis & Methodology

A serious exchange rate forecast should not lock in a single number, but rather establish a price range accompanied by probabilities. The reason is that USD/VND is simultaneously influenced by Vietnam's growth, domestic inflation, US interest rates and inflation, global trade, FDI flows, the current account balance, energy prices, and State Bank of Vietnam policy. Therefore, the most responsible approach is to build scenarios and update probabilities as new data emerges. All analysis below is based on data collected from authoritative sources and based on certain assumptions as well as data-driven inference.

Scenario Rationale

Why Scenario 2 (55%): Most consistent with both 10-year historical trend (~1.2%/yr, [1]) and current fundamentals. Vietnam maintains strong growth and external buffers, while the Fed has not signaled deep rate cuts ([5]). This combination typically produces moderate, controlled depreciation.

Why Scenario 1 (USD weakens) has low probability (20%): In terms of USD strength, it still has strong support: US interest rates and US inflation have not returned to ultra-low territory. The Fed's Summary of Economic Projections dated March 18, 2026 shows the median federal funds rate at 3.4% for 2026, 3.1% for 2027, and 3.1% for 2028. The same projections show US PCE inflation at 2.7%, 2.2%, and 2.0% for 2026–2028. In short: the Fed is telling markets it will cut rates, but not rapidly toward zero. This undermines the argument that 'USD will naturally fall sharply in the coming years'.

Furthermore, international organizations still see risks from global trade. The World Bank in their March 2025 report clearly states the largest uncertainty facing Vietnam's outlook comes from slower-than-expected global growth and trade disruptions, especially with major partners like the US, EU, and China. The IMF in September 2025 further emphasized that Vietnam's 2025–2026 outlook is constrained by 'elevated global uncertainty on trade policies' and the impact of 'new U.S. tariffs'.

Finally, domestic inflation remains an annual issue. The IMF projected Vietnam's average CPI at 3.4% for 2025 and 3.2% for 2026; newer NSO data shows March 2026 CPI rose 4.65% year-on-year and Q1 average CPI rose 3.51%. This means the Vietnam–US inflation differential is not sufficient to create a clear VND strengthening narrative. It leans more toward the outcome: VND depreciates gradually.

Why Scenario 3 (VND weakens) has low probability (25%): The first reason lies in Vietnam's external health. The IMF reports that in 2024, Vietnam achieved a record current account surplus of 6.6% of GDP; gross international reserves stand at approximately $83.1 billion; and the IMF emphasizes 2024 growth reached 7.1%. This is a very important buffer for the exchange rate. An economy with high growth, strong external surpluses, and significant foreign exchange reserves usually cannot walk straight into a chaotic depreciation scenario — unless there is a very powerful external shock.

The second reason is that actual 2025 data is much stronger than many expected in mid-2025. In March 2025, the World Bank forecast Vietnam would grow 6.8% in 2025 and 6.5% in 2026, while projecting FDI disbursement around $25 billion. But official NSO data subsequently showed 2025 GDP grew 8.02% — significantly above the earlier conservative forecasts. This is an important signal: Vietnam's shock absorption capacity is currently not weak.

The third reason is that in terms of policy management, Vietnam tends to let the exchange rate adjust gradually to absorb shocks, rather than necessarily holding a fixed level indefinitely. The IMF in their 2025 consultation stated quite clearly that 'greater exchange rate flexibility is critical' to help the economy adjust to external shocks.

How Scenarios Could Change

If between now and end of 2026 three signals appear simultaneously — Vietnam CPI falls more sustainably, the Fed moves below its current 3.1–3.4% trajectory, and trade risks ease — we would increase the probability of the USD-weakens scenario and decrease the VND-weakens scenario. Conversely, if energy prices escalate, transport costs rise, international trade deteriorates further, and Vietnam CPI remains elevated for several more quarters, we would raise the VND-weakens scenario probability above 30%.

Projection Methodology

The practical foundations for the forecast include 4 layers:

Layer 1: Historical trend — approximately 1.2%/year average depreciation in 2015–2024.

Layer 2: Inflation and interest rate differential — Vietnam is somewhat higher than the US, while the Fed has not quickly departed from positive real interest rate territory.

Layer 3: Vietnam's external buffers — growth, FDI, current account surplus, foreign exchange reserves.

Layer 4: Risk premium for contingencies such as trade, tariffs, energy, and transport.

Final Conclusions:

1. Ten years of data does not support the view that USD/VND will stand still; the fundamental trend remains USD gradually strengthening against VND.

2. Current data also does not support the view that 'USD will definitely surge above 30,000' as the primary scenario, because Vietnam still has strong growth, large current account surplus, significant foreign exchange reserves, and stable FDI.

3. The most reasonable approach for planning is to hold the base scenario of 27,600–28,800, assigned 55% probability. The entire logic is: rising, but rising moderately, with the risk of a sharper increase still large enough that one should not be complacent.

Note on methodology: A responsible exchange rate forecast must use ranges and probabilities — not a single number. USD/VND is simultaneously influenced by Vietnam's growth, domestic inflation, US interest rates and inflation, global trade flows, FDI, the current account balance, energy prices, and State Bank of Vietnam policy. The probability allocations (20%–55%–25%) are subjective assessments calibrated against the data currently available, not absolute mathematical probabilities. They should be updated as new data emerges.
