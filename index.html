Key observation: VND has depreciated gradually against USD as a long-term trend, but in a controlled, non-linear manner — not a sudden collapse. From 21,698 (2015) to 24,165 (2024), cumulative depreciation was ~11.4%, or approximately 1.2% per year annualized. Some years saw acceleration (2023: +2.2%), while others were nearly flat (2021: -0.2%).

Interpretation: Vietnam's economy remains strong (GDP 8.02% in 2025, record current account surplus). However, domestic inflation pressure persists — CPI in March 2026 hit 4.65% YoY (NSO [6]), and the Vietnam–US inflation differential does not support a VND appreciation story. Meanwhile, the Fed is signaling only gradual rate cuts (Fed SEP [5]), keeping USD structurally supported. The IMF notes that "greater exchange rate flexibility is critical" for Vietnam ([2]), implying continued managed depreciation rather than a fixed peg.

Why Scenario 2 (55%): Most consistent with both 10-year historical trend (~1.2%/yr, [1]) and current fundamentals. Vietnam maintains strong growth and external buffers, while the Fed has not signaled deep rate cuts ([5]). This combination typically produces moderate, controlled depreciation.

Why Scenario 1 is only 20%: Requires multiple favorable conditions simultaneously — sharper Fed easing than current SEP projections, reduced global trade tensions, and lower Vietnam CPI. Current data (CPI 4.65% in Mar 2026, [6]) does not support this.

Why Scenario 3 is 25%: Risks are material — IMF [2] highlights "elevated global uncertainty on trade policies" and "new U.S. tariffs" as binding constraints. World Bank [4] identifies trade disruptions as the largest downside risk. However, Vietnam's buffers ($83.1B reserves, 6.6% CA surplus) prevent this from being the base case.

Note on methodology: A responsible exchange rate forecast must use ranges and probabilities — not a single number. USD/VND is simultaneously influenced by Vietnam's growth, domestic inflation, US interest rates and inflation, global trade flows, FDI, the current account balance, energy prices, and State Bank of Vietnam policy. The probability allocations (20%–55%–25%) are subjective assessments calibrated against the data currently available, not absolute mathematical probabilities. They should be updated as new data emerges.
